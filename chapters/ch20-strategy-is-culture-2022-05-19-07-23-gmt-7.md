# Chapter 20: Strategy is Culture

## Core Idea
Teams organize around *mission* (permanent, unchanging) not *goals* (temporary, variable). True independence requires interdependence, which requires attraction—so the best strategic posture is empowering others ahead of your own launch.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Mission vs Goals**: Missions are ontological (never fully achieved); goals are instrumental (you reach them, then set new ones). Teams that anchor to missions stay aligned indefinitely.
  - When to use: When evaluating whether a team should merge, split, or stay together
  - How: Ask "Is our mission the same?" rather than "Do we agree on this quarter's target?"

- **P24 Acceptance Principle** (رسالت): "Every empowered squad empowers other squads to the extent possible."
  - When to use: As a primary evaluation metric for team success
  - How: Track compliments and referrals received, not goals scored

- **Interdependence → Independence** (Covey's sequence):
  1. Attraction (create pull)
  2. Interdependence (mutual reliance)
  3. Independence (true autonomy)
  - When to use: When building personal or organizational leverage
  - How: Give value before asking for it; don't mistake isolation for strength

- **Proactive Triangle** (from Seven Habits):
  1. Be Proactive (anticipate, don't react)
  2. Begin with the End in Mind (work backward)
  3. First Things First (prioritize ruthlessly)
  - These are self-mastery; they precede interpersonal habits.

- **Empowerment Networks** (Network Theory):
  - Power grows through relationships, not nodes
  - A single node that hoards power causes collective collapse
  - When to use: Designing org structures and incentive systems
  - How: Reward people for enabling others' success

## Key Concepts
- **Mission (رسالت)**: Permanent calling; unlike goals, it cannot be completed, so it provides lasting alignment
- **Goal (هدف)**: Temporary target; once achieved, it must be replaced
- **Attraction (اثرکشن)**: Creating pull toward you through value delivery before asking for anything
- **Interdependence**: Being mutually dependent such that one's gain creates the other's gain; prerequisite for real independence
- **Independence**: Self-sufficiency achieved *through* interdependence, not in spite of it
- **Synergy**: Combined output exceeds sum of parts; requires win-win mindset
- **Seek First to Understand**: Listen before speaking; most people try to persuade instead
- **Sharmad-e-Sazesh (Compromise)**: Not the same as synergy; synergy finds better solutions, compromise settles for less
- **Empowerment**: Enabling others; the mechanism by which networks grow

## Mental Models
- The **Poacher's Dilemma**: If you truly believe another's work matters more than yours, the rational move is to help—not to compete or start something new
- **Spotify's Compliment Culture**: People praise colleagues, not themselves; this reverses the natural hierarchy of recognition
- **Ecosystem Logic**: No person or organization can satisfy all needs alone; success comes from creating more capacity in the system, not extracting more value
- **Referral Flywheel**: Giving three free trials creates more trust than one paid transaction; the initial loss compounds into network growth

## Anti-patterns
- **Goal-chasing**: Teams that reorient every season because goals changed, not mission
- **Schism culture**: "Three people make a party, two make a faction"—splitting whenever disagreement arises
- **Competition reflex**: Assuming anyone ahead of you is inferior and must be outdone rather than learned from
- **Isolation as strength**: Mistaking independence for solitude; true independence requires interdependence first
- **Self-praise**: Only praising oneself while expecting others to notice; breaks the compliment flywheel

## Key Takeaways
1. Anchor teams to mission, not goals—missions don't expire, so alignment persists
2. If you believe someone else's work is more important, join them rather than start over
3. Build interdependence through attraction (giving value before taking)
4. Measure success by empowerment received, not goals scored
5. Win-win thinking and seeking first to understand are prerequisites, not add-ons, to independent action
6. Organizational design should reward people who enable others, not just those who deliver output
