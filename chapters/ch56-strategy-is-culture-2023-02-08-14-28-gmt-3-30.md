# Chapter 56: Strategy is Culture

## Core Idea
Content repurposing is a strategic weapon when guided by clear intention ("peps"). Platforms that understand how to adapt existing content for new audiences and purposes win over those stuck producing everything from scratch.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Peps (Intention)**: Every piece of content should have a conscious purpose behind it. When you know your *peps*, you can repurpose intelligently rather than randomly reshuffling.
  - When to use: Before creating or redistributing any content asset
  - How: Ask "what am I actually trying to achieve?" and align format/platform choices accordingly

- **Access Barriers Framework**: Consumers won't engage due to five types of friction — **Time** (don't have enough), **Skill** (can't understand), **Scale** (too small to matter), **Resource** (lack tools), and **Money**. Suppliers face similar barriers.
  - When to use: Diagnosing why a segment isn't consuming your content
  - How: Map each barrier to a specific repurposing action (e.g., translate for *skill*, summarize for *time*)

- **Content Repurpose vs. Content Production**: Repurposing means taking existing content and transforming it for a different intent/platform. Production means starting from zero. Repurposing is often strategically superior.
  - When to use: Scaling content output without proportional effort
  - How: Identify high-signal assets, then adapt format, audience, or platform

- **Platform Unbundling**: Don't try to build a full platform; unbundle it and focus on where you create unique value. Examples: Glassdoor unbagaged LinkedIn's hiring value; TikTok unbagaged YouTube's discovery.
  - When to use: Entering markets dominated by full-stack platforms
  - How: Find the sub-value a big platform does well but incompletely, and specialize there

## Key Concepts
- **Peps (قصد)**: Conscious intention or purpose behind an action; the "heart" of why you're doing something
- **Content Repurpose**: Adapting content with a different goal than original — not just redistribution
- **Gatekeeper**: A platform controlling access to content (regulatory term); Google is the archetypal internet gatekeeper
- **Default Search Engine**: The battle over what appears as default matters more than organic search quality
- **Network Effect via Sharing**: Creating value by letting others see what you've saved/curated (Glassdoor's model)
- **Platform Unbundling**: Breaking apart a full-stack platform to capture a specific layer of value
- **Repurposing Anti-Patterns**: SEO farms, scraped content with no added intent — these are not true repurposing

## Mental Models
- **Google is always the implicit competitor**: Every search-related product is implicitly competing with Google's default position. Even products that don't look like search engines (Reddit, TikTok, Instagram) are positioning against Google's discovery function.
- **Content is a multiplicative asset**: One piece of valuable content, properly repurposed across formats and intents, creates disproportionate value compared to producing equivalent new content.
- **Incumbents are never truly defeated, only shifted**: Google's ad revenue growth has plateaued because internet penetration is saturated, but Google defends through Shopping, Flights, Photos, Maps — leveraging assets competitors can't easily replicate.
- **Innovation is required for sustained growth**: Even dominant players must continuously reinvent; stagnation leads to vulnerability regardless of current market share.

## Anti-patterns
- **Treating all repurposing as redistribution**: Simply posting a blog as a tweet or podcast is not true repurposing — it ignores the *peps* and doesn't solve for the new audience's constraints
- **Competing head-on with Google on their turf**: New entrants fail when they try to build "better search" rather than finding adjacent value layers (discovery, trust networks, visual search)
- **Ignoring the purpose behind content**: Creating content without a clear *peps* leads to random distribution that misses audience constraints
- **SEO scraping as repurposing**: Copying dead sites' content with no added intent is not strategic repurposing — it's a content farm tactic that won't build lasting value

## Key Takeaways
1. Every piece of content should start with a clear *peps* — intention shapes repurposing decisions
2. Map audience barriers (time, skill, scale, resource, money) to specific repurposing actions
3. Focus on content repurposing, not content production, when scaling output
4. Enter incumbent-dominated markets by unbundling — find one layer of value they do incompletely
5. All search-adjacent products are implicitly competing with Google's default position
6. Incumbents defend through assets competitors can't replicate (network effects, data moats, infrastructure)
7. Sustained innovation is non-negotiable — even winners must keep reinventing
