# Chapter 54: Strategy is Culture

## Core Idea
Global strategy isn't about exporting a local product—it's about designing for affordances that transcend geography from day one. True global products (Born Global) abstract away location-specific assumptions, while multinationals often mask their origins. The key lever is building **API-level affordances** that become infrastructure others build upon.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Affordance Decomposition**: Strip away location, channel, and journey specifics from a product concept until you reach interaction primitives that work universally (search, profile, review, booking). These affordances are location-independent.
  - When to use: When evaluating whether a product idea can scale beyond its original market context
  - How: Take the product apart into interaction units; test each against "does this depend on geography?"

- **API as Infrastructure**: Products that expose APIs become layers in a broader ecosystem. The more a product behaves like an API, the more it becomes a building block for others rather than just a standalone tool.
  - When to use: When deciding how to architect a product for maximum leverage
  - How: Identify which parts of your system others might want to integrate with, then expose them cleanly

- **Orchestration over Creation**: You don't need to invent everything. Build value by recombining existing technologies, networks, and capabilities into new arrangements. Apple didn't invent GPS, digital cameras, or touchscreens—they orchestrated them into iPhone.
  - When to use: When planning product scope or competitive positioning
  - How: Map what already exists; identify novel combinations that create new value

- **Interdependence → Independence**: Use existing networks and properties aggressively, but avoid becoming dependent on them. The goal is to leverage others' systems while maintaining your own freedom.
  - When to use: When deciding partnerships, integrations, or platform dependencies
  - How: Ask "can I build the same capability elsewhere?" before committing

## Key Concepts
- **Born Global vs. Multinational**: Born Global products are designed with location-independence from inception. Multinationals (like Philips) actively obscure their geographic origins to appear local everywhere—a deliberate anti-localization strategy.

- **Location Independence**: Designing products whose core value doesn't depend on being rooted in a specific geography, culture, or legal jurisdiction.

- **Network Effects**: When a product's value increases as more people use it. Deel's hiring platform gains strength because each employer and employee added makes it more valuable for others.

- **Switching Costs**: What prevents users from leaving. Deel embeds itself in payroll, benefits, and compliance workflows—making it expensive and painful to switch.

- **Unbundling / Re-bundling**: Companies can unbundle themselves from traditional employment relationships (as Deel attempts by offering benefits directly to workers independent of employers), then re-bundle around new value propositions.

- **API Economy**: The shift from monolithic products to modular services that compose into larger systems.

## Mental Models
- **The Platform Trap**: A product that looks great in isolation may fail if it doesn't become part of something bigger. Deel succeeds not as a hiring tool but as an integration layer into payroll, HR, and compliance ecosystems.

- **The Deel Example**: Deel didn't invent hiring or remote work—it took one piece of the stack, made it globally available, and kept adding features. Its real moat isn't the product itself but the network effects and switching costs that accumulate.

- **Location Is a Constraint, Not a Strategy**: Starting in San Jose for Russian speakers (WhatsApp) was a tactical choice, not a strategic limitation. Decompose to affordances, then recombine globally.

- **Independence Through Interdependence**: The paradox that to be truly independent, you must first learn to use others' systems well—then build your own escape hatches.

## Anti-patterns
- **Feature Bloat Without Network Effects**: Adding 100 features to a local product without making it globally useful. Features don't compound without network effects.
- **Hiding Origins as a Strategy**: Philips spending decades trying to make you forget it's Dutch. This works temporarily but creates a fundamental trust gap—no one knows where they stand.
- **Building Everything Yourself**: The mistake of thinking you need to invent every component. Most innovation happens at the composition layer, not the invention layer.
- **Staying Local Because It's Easier**: The comfort of designing for one market's regulations, culture, and behaviors instead of decomposing to universal affordances.

## Key Takeaways
1. Decompose products to their affordances—these are the universal primitives that transcend geography.
2. Born Global means designing for location independence from day one, not expanding locally first.
3. APIs are the new infrastructure—products that expose clean APIs become building blocks for others.
4. Orchestration beats invention: most breakthrough products rearrange existing technology, not invent it.
5. Build network effects and switching costs deliberately—these are your real moats, not features.
6. Use interdependence strategically; maintain independence by always having alternatives.
7. The best products make you feel the service is local, even when it isn't—but the underlying architecture is global.
