# Chapter 90: "You" are the culture

## Core Idea
To build a sustainable startup, you must invest your time in recruiting and developing long-term thinkers (Infinite Game players), creating a team "vortex" that attracts people through shared purpose and psychological safety rather than transactional salaries.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Vesting & Cliff**: A commitment mechanism where equity vests over ~4 years, with a "cliff" (usually 1 year) where leaving early yields zero shares. 
  - When to use: Structuring co-founder and early employee agreements to align with 10-15 year horizons.
  - How: Use it to filter out short-term thinkers; VCs invest in people willing to wait 4+ years for equity, not just ideas.
- **Invitation vs. Application**: Shifting the psychological frame from asking people to "apply" to personally inviting them to "join."
  - When to use: Recruiting early employees, interns, or partners.
  - How: Leverage your existing network and communities to issue direct invitations, reducing anxiety and fostering commitment.
- **The Vortex Team**: A team dynamic that pulls people in through interpersonal gravity rather than financial incentives.
  - When to use: Building early-stage teams where cash is limited.
  - How: Cultivate a group where people want to spend their 8-hour workdays alongside you, alleviating their existential anxiety about the future.

## Key Concepts
- **Infinite Game**: Operating with the assumption that the game never ends; even if one project concludes, another begins, requiring 10-15 year thinking.
- **Risk Premium**: The extra compensation demanded by senior talent to leave a stable job for a risky startup.
- **Transactional Mindset**: A hiring approach where you pay above-market rates to poach talent, resulting in employees who leave at the first better offer.
- **Anxiety Loop**: A cycle where lack of commitment leads to lack of focus, resulting in no concrete achievements, which in turn heightens anxiety.
- **Information Gap**: The disparity in knowledge between parties (e.g., a driver vs. a buyer of illicit goods), often exploited through scams and ripe for platformization.
- **Time as Capital**: The reality that your time is your most valuable asset; failing to invest it in team-building forces you to dilute equity later.

## Mental Models
- People don't join startups for ideas or purpose; they join for the team and the leader.
- If you hire for today's tasks, you cannot build tomorrow's organization.
- Flawed philosophy inevitably leads to flawed execution.
- Trust and value are created by bridging information gaps and solving concrete problems.

## Anti-patterns
- **Paying Above Market for Seniors**: Offering more than established companies (like Digikala) to attract talent. This burns cash and attracts transactional players who treat the startup as a stepping stone.
- **Hiring for Today's Tasks**: Recruiting staff merely to clear the current backlog rather than finding individuals who will grow into leadership roles over 10-20 years.
- **Asking People to Apply**: Using generic job postings instead of personal invitations, which increases psychological friction and signals a lack of genuine commitment.

## Key Takeaways
1. Hire co-founders and early employees with a 10-15 year horizon, not just for short-term task completion.
2. Your existing network is your primary recruitment channel; personally invite people to "join" rather than making them "apply."
3. Build a team that acts as a "vortex"—pulling people in because they want to work with you, reducing their anxiety about uncertainty.
4. Avoid paying risk premiums above market rates for senior hires; it attracts transactional mindsets and burns cash.
5. Your time is your most valuable asset—invest it in team development before raising external funding and giving away equity.
6. Identify and bridge "information gaps" to validate business opportunities (e.g., creating trust in opaque markets).
7. Ensure your underlying philosophy aligns with your actions; flawed philosophy leads to flawed execution.
