# Chapter 46: Strategy is Culture

## Core Idea
Global strategy isn't about picking a geography—it's about extracting universal affordances from local contexts and building infrastructure platforms that empower third-party innovation.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Genralization**: Don't anchor your product to a specific geography; identify shared human needs that exist across regions.
  - When to use: Launching a product intended for international scale
  - How: Extract common features, strip out local-specific assumptions, rebuild around universal patterns

- **Affordance**: What a platform enables a user to do, independent of industry context (search, booking, listing, reviews, ratings).
  - When to use: Designing platform foundations
  - How: Ask "what actions does this enable?" rather than "what industry problem does this solve?"

- **Infrastructure Software vs. Application Software**: Software that provides capabilities enabling *others* to build applications, rather than doing the work itself.
  - When to use: Building foundational layers (databases, cloud compute, PaaS)
  - How: Provide composable primitives; let third parties compose them

- **Innovation Platform**: A platform whose primary job is to enable and empower third-party developers to create value.
  - When to use: Scaling beyond what a single team can build
  - How: Invest in SDKs, APIs, documentation, and developer incentives

- **Third-Party Applications**: Solutions built *on top of* an innovation platform that address industry-specific needs the platform owner doesn't serve.
  - When to use: Filling vertical gaps without building them yourself
  - How: Expose platform capabilities; let partners build the specialization

- **Chicken-and-Egg Problem**: The classic platform challenge—neither side joins until the other side exists.
  - When to use: Any marketplace, network, or platform launch
  - How: Subsidize one side (supply or demand), use standards for compatibility, or seed with trusted early adopters

## Key Concepts
- **Genralization**: Moving beyond geography as a binding factor; finding what's truly universal across markets
- **Affordance**: The set of capabilities a platform exposes, independent of vertical context
- **Infrastructure Software**: Software that provides reusable capabilities rather than end-user functionality
- **Innovation Platform**: A platform designed to enable third-party creation rather than direct consumption
- **Third-Party Application**: Industry-specific solution built atop a common platform
- **Chicken-and-Egg Problem**: The coordination challenge of getting two sides of a market to join simultaneously
- **Subsidization Strategy**: Incentivizing one side of a market to jump-start network effects
- **Compatibility Standard**: Technical specification enabling cross-platform interoperability (POSIX, Stripe)
- **Portable Application**: Software designed to run across different platforms without modification
- **Local Starting Point**: Beginning a venture in a familiar geography without letting it constrain global ambition

## Mental Models
- **Start Local, Think Global**: You can begin where you're comfortable, but your ambition must transcend geography. An app serving 2-3M users in Iran is "in the sky"—think in billions, not millions.
- **Platform > Marketplace > Social Network > Consumer App**: The most scalable products are innovation platforms because they multiply value through third-party creation.
- **Network Effects Require Coordination**: Every platform faces the chicken-and-egg problem; solving it often means subsidizing the side that's harder to attract.
- **Standards Create Ecosystems**: POSIX enabled Linux by making Unix apps portable; Stripe enables cloud apps by standardizing payments. Standards are invisible infrastructure.

## Anti-patterns
- **Geography as Identity**: Treating "for the Middle East" as a product definition rather than a starting context. Marginalized/underserved segments exist globally—Africa, Southeast Asia, Latin America.
- **Building Everything Yourself**: Attempting to solve both infrastructure and application problems internally limits scale and slows iteration.
- **Waiting for Perfect Product**: Iteration is necessary; Linux succeeded despite being incomplete, because it had enough to matter.
- **Ignoring the Chicken-and-Egg Problem**: Assuming both sides will appear simultaneously guarantees failure.

## Key Takeaways
1. Your starting point can be local, but your strategy shouldn't be constrained by it—find universal affordances.
2. The most ambitious products are innovation platforms (Shopify, Stripe, AWS, Linux) because they multiply value through third-party ecosystems.
3. Infrastructure software solves the "common needs" problem; third-party apps solve the "industry-specific needs" problem.
4. Solve the chicken-and-egg problem early—subsidize the harder side, use standards for compatibility, or seed with credible early adopters.
5. POSIX compatibility made Linux viable; Stripe compatibility makes cloud apps viable. Standards are invisible moats.
6. Start with what works locally, iterate relentlessly, and expand your ambition faster than your geography expands.
7. The best product teams don't wait for perfection—they ship, learn, and iterate. Linux wasn't complete; it was usable. That was enough.
