# Chapter 6: Strategy is Culture (2022-01-12 at 04:29 GMT-8)

## Core Idea
Building a large-scale product is a *people problem*, not a technical one. You need thousands of engineers, designers, and operators—not because the code is complex, but because every feature requires dedicated craft. Strategy cannot outpace culture; if your organization doesn't hire and organize like a modern product company, no amount of budget or willpower will help.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **First Principles Thinking (Elon Musk)**: Strip away inherited assumptions; rebuild from fundamental truths.
  - When to use: When an industry or organization is stuck in inherited conventions.
  - How: Ask "What must be true?" instead of "How did we always do this?"

- **T-Shaped Person**
  - When to use: Hiring senior talent for ambiguous, cross-domain challenges.
  - How: Seek candidates who once achieved extreme depth in one area, then deliberately switched domains and learned again. Depth teaches them *how* to go deep; breadth lets them connect across domains.

- **Nuclear Power Plant Analogy**
  - When to use: Explaining why startups can't scale without massive human infrastructure.
  - How: Just as you can't build a reactor without uranium, you can't run a million-user product with 55 engineers. Scale requires scale in people.

## Key Concepts
- **Synergy vs. Specialization**: A small group of well-aligned generalists outperforms siloed specialists.
- **Jihadi Entrepreneurship**: Forward motion without a complete plan ("tell me where to go and I'll get there").
- **Jihadi Resistance**: Saying "it can't be done" as a defense mechanism against change.
- **Syndrome of Unsatisfied Talent**: Promoting a good performer into more work until they burn out, rather than hiring additional people.
- **Cross-functional Squads**: Small teams (roughly pizza-sized) containing all roles needed for a feature end-to-end.
- **Feature Factory**: Organizations that keep adding features without changing how they operate.
- **Deep Learning as Primary Skill**: The most valuable competency in a rapidly shifting landscape.
- **Product Before Marketing**: Build something genuinely good; distribution follows.

## Mental Models
- **Scale changes the game**: 55-person Instagram cannot survive without 8,850 engineers behind WhatsApp; the math forces a sale.
- **Specialization is a trap in uncertainty**: The best decision-makers today are generalists who can learn faster than domain experts.
- **Institutions protect themselves**: Universities, hospitals, and ministries resist innovation not because they're dumb, but because their incentives reward continuity.
- **Culture precedes strategy**: You cannot execute a digital strategy with government hiring practices.

## Anti-patterns
- **"We'll Do It Ourselves" Syndrome**: State organizations that refuse to buy or partner, believing they can replicate private-sector velocity internally.
- **Over-promoting high performers**: Assigning more work to good employees instead of hiring, creating burnout and stagnation.
- **Waterfall thinking for creative work**: Applying linear, phase-gated processes to domains that require iteration and discovery.
- **Hiring specialists for ambiguous problems**: Experts in one domain struggle to synthesize across domains; they miss what generalists see.
- **Scaling features without scaling people**: Adding features faster than you add the humans who design, test, and maintain them.

## Key Takeaways
1. Every major feature needs dedicated people—design, development, testing. A single feature is not free.
2. The best hires are not current-domain experts but former-deep-specialists who can learn anything quickly.
3. Organizational structure must match ambition: small cross-functional squads inside autonomous units.
4. "It can't be done" is often resistance dressed as realism; the real question is whether you've hired the right people.
5. Product excellence drives distribution; marketing and sales are secondary to building something remarkable.
6. Government organizations face structural barriers (hiring limits, procurement) that no amount of willpower can overcome.
7. The hardest skill in today's world is *learning how to learn*—not domain-specific knowledge.
