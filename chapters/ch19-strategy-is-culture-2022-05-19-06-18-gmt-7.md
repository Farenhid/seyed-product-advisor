# Chapter 19: Strategy is Culture

## Core Idea
Stop using OKRs to align teams—alignment comes from shared principles. Focus on solving one pin (a single real problem) with a small team that moves fast. Culture and speed matter more than scale.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Management by Principles vs Management by Objectives**: OKRs are wrong for alignment because people treat them as targets to hit, not problems to solve. Instead, define shared principles so everyone understands *how* to decide.
  - When to use: Early-stage companies building culture, not scaling
  - How: Replace OKR documents with principle documents; measure whether people act autonomously on those principles

- **Pin vs Domain**: A domain is a broad area (e.g., "doctors," "patients"). A pin is a specific, solvable problem within that area. Spreading effort across domains creates activity without impact.
  - When to use: When teams are fragmented across multiple initiatives
  - How: Pick one pin, commit to it, resist the urge to add more

- **Quickness as Primary Metric**: Speed of execution is the core organizational capability—not revenue, not growth, just getting things done faster.
  - When to use: When teams are slow despite having good plans
  - How: Make "quickness" the first objective; everything else follows

- **Design Thinking (Internal)**: Use design thinking not for products but for fixing how the team itself works—its processes, communication, and collaboration.
  - When to use: When the team is stuck in bad habits
  - How: Treat internal friction as a design problem; prototype solutions

## Key Concepts
- **Alignment through principles**: People who believe in the principles act on their own; no need to keep reminding them
- **Culture fit layoffs**: Fire for cultural misfit purposefully, not as a painkiller when money runs out
- **Small teams > Large teams**: A 30-person team focused on one pin beats a 60-person team spread across domains
- **Children mindset**: Learn from children's simplicity and lack of ego, not from large established companies
- **Internal customers**: Treat colleagues as customers too, not just external users
- **Engagement loop**: Create feedback loops that make doctors want to return (e.g., showing search result positions)
- **Status Quo Challenge**: Question why you're doing something just because others do it
- **Principle-first thinking**: Start with the problem, not the solution

## Mental Models
- Don't wait for permission or leadership approval to act—autonomy drives culture
- Large companies copy big companies; small companies should learn from children's clarity
- When you're solving one pin, every decision gets clearer; when you're solving many, nothing gets solved
- Documents don't change behavior—belief does. Only after belief forms do documents become useful

## Anti-patterns
- **OKR-driven alignment**: Leads to target-hitting instead of problem-solving; wastes time writing and re-reading documents
- **Expanding team size during uncertainty**: Hiring/firing for budget reasons destroys culture; intentional downsizing for agility is different
- **Copying big companies**: Practices from DiDi, Digikala, or Divar don't apply to early-stage teams
- **Multiple domains**: Trying to solve patient, doctor, and hospital problems simultaneously dilutes focus
- **Treating colleagues as competitors**: Internal politics and self-promotion create negative feedback loops
- **Waiting for the perfect strategy**: Months spent debating one pin is expensive but necessary

## Key Takeaways
1. Stop using OKRs for alignment—use principles instead
2. Focus on one pin, not multiple domains
3. Build a small team that moves fast; speed is the primary metric
4. Solve internal problems with design thinking before external ones
5. Fire intentionally for culture fit, not as a financial painkiller
6. Learn from children's simplicity, not from large companies' complexity
7. Belief changes behavior; documents follow belief, they don't create it
