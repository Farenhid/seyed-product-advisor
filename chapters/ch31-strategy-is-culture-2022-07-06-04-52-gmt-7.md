# Chapter 31: Strategy is Culture

## Core Idea
Digital strategy is fundamentally about culture—understanding how people already behave and riding those existing networks and habits rather than fighting the status quo. Build on what's already happening, not against it.

## Frameworks Introduced

- **Ride the Network Effect**: Platforms should distribute through existing social networks (Instagram, Telegram, etc.) rather than building new ones from scratch. Every major startup succeeds by surfacing on someone else's network.
  - When to use: Choosing distribution strategy, deciding whether to build or borrow audience
  - How: Identify which existing networks your users already inhabit and optimize for that surface

- **10× Better Than Offline**: The real competition isn't other online companies—it's the offline alternative. Your product must be 10× better than what people currently do.
  - When to use: Evaluating product-market fit, competitive positioning
  - How: Ask "what offline behavior does this replace?" and benchmark against that, not against rivals

- **Exponential Metrics**: Track metrics that compound—network size, internal traffic share, liquidity—not linear inputs like ad spend.
  - When to use: Selecting KPIs, evaluating growth trajectory
  - How: Choose indicators that grow faster as the network gets larger

- **Supply-Demand Dynamics**: Most marketplace failures are supply-side, not demand-side. Build supply before chasing demand.
  - When to use: Marketplace launch planning, scaling decisions
  - How: Ensure supply is ready before investing heavily in demand generation

## Key Concepts

- **Internal Traffic Share**: The percentage of visitors coming from your own network vs. external channels. Higher share = lower marketing dependency.
- **Switching Cost**: The friction preventing users from leaving your platform. Low switching costs weaken network effects.
- **Feed Investment Model**: Prioritize building engaging content experiences over paid marketing. Users stay longer when the feed delivers value.
- **Multi-homing**: Users spreading across multiple similar platforms. Indicates weak network effects and low switching costs.
- **Capacity**: The maximum useful engagement time a platform can provide (e.g., Uber's ~500M transaction capacity).
- **Supply Liquidity**: How quickly listed suppliers get matched with buyers. Faster = higher conversion.
- **Match Volume**: Total number of successful buyer-seller connections made on the platform.
- **Quality Curation**: Choosing fewer, higher-quality suppliers over sheer volume to increase match probability.
- **Retention as Leading Indicator**: Retention signals future health before lagging metrics like revenue show it.
- **Network Value Correction**: As perceived network value rises, users who previously churned return.
- **Content Engine**: The recommendation algorithm/feed infrastructure powering content products.
- **Basalam Case Study**: A marketplace that failed because it assumed artisans wanted full e-commerce tools when they actually needed help with existing workflows (bulk ordering, wholesale distribution).

## Mental Models

- **Networks compound, not add**: Growth compounds exponentially when users create value for other users
- **Status quo is the default competitor**: People don't switch unless the alternative is dramatically better
- **Supply enables demand**: You can't sell what doesn't exist
- **Context drives engagement**: Communication works best within existing contexts (e.g., messaging a friend) rather than in isolation

## Anti-patterns

- **Competitor obsession**: Watching competitors instead of the offline status quo. You'll never catch them; the real opportunity is replacing offline behavior.
- **NPS chasing**: Optimizing for Net Promoter Score without fixing actual engagement problems. NPS is a lagging indicator.
- **Supply neglect**: Building demand before ensuring supply. Marketplaces die on the supply side.
- **Feature bloat**: Adding features instead of solving the core constraint. Basalam tried to handle everything from makeup to sales—focus on the one thing that unlocks the bottleneck.
- **Lagging-only metrics**: Waiting for revenue decline to diagnose retention problems. Use retention as a leading indicator.

## Key Takeaways

1. Always distribute through existing networks rather than building new ones
2. Benchmark against offline alternatives, not online competitors
3. Choose exponential metrics (liquidity, match volume) over linear ones (ad spend, page views)
4. Solve supply-side problems before demand-side problems
5. Reduce switching costs and multi-homing through network effects
6. Build content engines, not just content—recommendation systems compound engagement
7. Use retention as a leading indicator, not a post-mortem metric
8. Curate quality over quantity in supply acquisition
