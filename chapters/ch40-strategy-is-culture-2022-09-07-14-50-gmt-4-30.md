# Chapter 40: Build Globally From Anywhere

## Core Idea
Globalization is not about geography—it's about jurisdiction independence. You can build world-class products from anywhere, but building a company requires strategic decisions about legal structure and headquarters that are separate from product development.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Globalization ≠ Geography**: True globalization means location-independent operations. Your product can ship worldwide regardless of where you sit; your company registration and HQ choices are separate strategic decisions.
  - When to use: When evaluating whether to expand internationally or whether location constrains your ambitions.
  - How: Separate the question of *who uses your product* from *where your legal entity sits*.

- **Build Product vs Build Company**: These are distinct activities with different constraints. You can build a world-class product from Cyprus, Serbia, or Tehran. Building a company involves legal structures, fundraising paths, and regulatory considerations that favor certain jurisdictions (e.g., Delaware C-Corp for US VC access, London for European capital).
  - When to use: When deciding where to incorporate, raise capital, or establish HQ.
  - How: Choose jurisdiction based on investor preferences and regulatory environment, not talent or quality-of-life factors.

- **Platform Economics**: A platform exists when the economic value of all users on it exceeds the economic value of the company that created it. This shifts focus from capturing value to enabling others to capture value.
  - When to use: When designing product architecture or deciding whether to open up to third-party developers.
  - How: Measure success by third-party value creation, not just your own revenue.

- **Network Effects Hierarchy**: Network effects compound in layers—from the simplest (protocol interoperability) to complex (multi-sided markets). Each layer adds stickiness and defensibility.
  - When to use: When thinking about competitive moats and product strategy.
  - How: Start with protocol/API-level interoperability, then add marketplace effects, then multi-sided dynamics.

## Key Concepts
- **Jurisdiction Independence**: The ability to operate globally regardless of physical location; decoupling where you build from where you sell.
- **Protocol-as-Standard**: When an API becomes the de facto standard (e.g., Amazon S3), switching costs approach zero for competitors adopting it, creating powerful network effects.
- **Innovation Platform**: A platform where the economic value of third-party creators/developers exceeds the platform owner's value—a much harder achievement than consumer network effects.
- **Chicken-and-Egg Solved by Global Reach**: Starting global circumvents the classic cold-start problem because you can address multiple sides of the market simultaneously across geographies.
- **Burnout Tolerance Gap**: Builders often fail not from lack of ability but because their ambitions exceed their sustainable capacity—duration matters more than intensity.
- **Long-Term Product Mindset**: Great products take 10+ years; quarterly roadmaps kill vision. Think in decades, not quarters.
- **Status Quo Challenge**: Surrounding yourself with diverse perspectives forces you to defend beliefs and think harder—homogeneous teams stagnate mentally.

## Mental Models
- **Location ≠ Capability**: Where you register a company matters for fundraising; where you build a product matters for talent and culture. These are independent decisions.
- **Open Source = Distributed Innovation**: Sharing code or APIs multiplies adoption without multiplying your effort—Linux grew faster than Android because applications pre-existed the OS.
- **Regulatory Arbitrage as Strategy**: Outsourcing compliance-heavy functions to specialists (e.g., legal tech, licensed payment processors) lets you move faster than incumbents bound by their own regulatory baggage.
- **Weekly Learning Cadence**: Short, frequent check-ins with mentors or peers create compounding learning that beats sporadic deep dives—consistency compounds.

## Anti-patterns
- **Building in Isolation**: Homogeneous teams around you create echo chambers that slow mental growth and blind you to global opportunities.
- **Ignoring API Strategy Until Too Late**: Most startups postpone thinking about APIs until they've built lock-in, then find themselves unable to compete with protocol-level players like Infobip.
- **Confusing Products With Platforms**: Having users ≠ being a platform. The distinction is whether third parties generate more value than you do.
- **Linear Wealth Thinking**: Believing wealth comes from saving or salary ignores that real wealth in modern economies comes from equity, ownership, and compounding assets.
- **Skipping Regulatory Groundwork**: Assuming regulations will stay static or that you can deal with them later leads to costly pivots when policy changes hit.
- **Overpromising Burnout Capacity**: Choosing ventures with ambition far beyond your sustainable energy level guarantees failure—not from lack of skill, but from exhaustion.

## Key Takeaways
1. Globalization is about jurisdiction independence, not Silicon Valley or Amsterdam—build wherever you have advantage.
2. Separate product-building from company-building; choose legal structure based on investor access, not lifestyle.
3. Platform economics means measuring success by what users and developers create, not what you capture.
4. Start thinking about API strategy on day one—protocol-level interoperability creates the deepest moats.
5. Great products are 10-year projects; resist quarterly pressure to ship incrementally toward a distant vision.
6. Diversify your intellectual circle deliberately—global ambitions require exposure to different cultures and backgrounds.
7. Burnout is a planning failure, not a character flaw—align ambition with sustainable capacity.
