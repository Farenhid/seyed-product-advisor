# Chapter 41: Strategy is Culture

## Core Idea
Global thinking is a mindset, not a geographic expansion—cultivating a worldwide perspective changes how you design products, communicate, and make strategic decisions even when operating locally.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Go-Global Mindset**: Reframe "going global" as thinking globally, acting locally. It's not about registering offshore companies or opening foreign bank accounts.
  - When to use: Any product decision where cross-cultural or cross-border patterns could improve outcomes
  - How: Hire diverse talent, expose teams to other markets, think in terms of global problem domains

- **Localization**: Make user experience dynamic to context—time, location, cultural norms, and language. Not just translation; contextual adaptation.
  - When to use: Designing interfaces, journeys, or services for diverse audiences
  - How: Treat geography and time as variables in your product architecture

- **Marketplace Liquidity**: The single most important metric for marketplaces; whoever reaches liquidity first dominates. Global thinking expands the search space for matching supply and demand.
  - When to use: Evaluating marketplace opportunities or growth strategies
  - How: Think beyond local borders when solving matching problems

## Key Concepts
- **Global Mindset**: A way of thinking that transcends geographic boundaries, enabling broader problem-solving and solution discovery
- **Localization**: Context-aware product design that adapts to local conditions while maintaining global coherence
- **Hard Currency**: Currencies that retain value (e.g., USD, EUR) vs. volatile local currencies; affects business model economics
- **Liquidity**: The ease with which buyers and sellers match in a marketplace
- **Network Effects**: Value increases as more participants join
- **Regulatory Strategy**: Using scale and speed to negotiate with regulators rather than being constrained by them
- **Product-Market Fit**: Prerequisite before raising capital or scaling aggressively
- **Public Speaking**: Critical skill for founders; correlates strongly with fundraising success
- **RFC (Request for Comments)**: Internet's decentralized protocol development process—no formal standardization body, adoption driven by usage
- **Status Quo Challenge**: Questioning accepted assumptions about how things work (e.g., "going global = moving abroad")

## Mental Models
- Your thoughts translate directly to your actions—people observe behavior, not claims. Think globally → act globally, even in small ways.
- A larger search space for solutions emerges when you think globally; problems become easier to solve.
- Network effects compound advantage in marketplaces—speed to liquidity matters more than perfect planning.
- Communication is foundational to startup success; invest in it early before it compounds negatively.

## Anti-patterns
- **Confusing global mindset with geographic expansion**: Registering foreign companies, opening offshore accounts, attending international events ≠ thinking globally
- **Over-capitalizing without product-market fit**: Scaling prematurely destroys options and signals weakness to investors
- **Ignoring localization**: Treating all users identically across cultures and contexts
- **Waiting for regulatory approval before building**: Scale creates leverage in regulatory negotiations; starting too conservatively limits growth
- **Building for the status quo**: Accepting current market structures without questioning whether they should exist

## Key Takeaways
1. Global thinking starts internally—it's a mental discipline, not a logistical exercise
2. Localization makes products context-aware; treat geography and time as first-class design parameters
3. Marketplace success hinges on liquidity, not features; think globally to widen matching surfaces
4. Public speaking ability strongly predicts fundraising success—practice it relentlessly
5. Hard currency exposure matters deeply in volatile economies like Iran
6. Don't raise capital before achieving product-market fit; it constrains future flexibility
7. Regulatory strategy favors bold, scaled players—not cautious, small ones
