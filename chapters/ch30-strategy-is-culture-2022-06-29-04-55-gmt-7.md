# Chapter 30: Strategy is Culture

## Core Idea
The core strategic insight of this session: **product expansion without corresponding discovery investment is value destruction**, and the difference between a platform and a project company ultimately comes down to how relationships between users generate compounding value.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Product Expansion Trap**: Adding SKUs/products without improving discovery dilutes the core value proposition. When search results become a sea of indistinguishable items, the discovery mechanism breaks.
  - When to use: Diagnose why growing your catalog isn't improving customer outcomes
  - How: Audit whether your search/discovery layer scales proportionally with inventory

- **Data Network Effect**: Unlike traditional network effects (which require people to connect to people), data network effects compound when each additional user contributes data that improves the ranking or recommendation engine for all others.
  - When to use: Building ranking, recommendation, or matching systems
  - How: Structure the product so each user's participation makes the algorithm better for everyone else

- **Platform vs Project Company**: A platform is defined by relationships between humans; a project company is defined by outputs (a single game, a single service). The two have fundamentally different economics, team structures, and investor profiles.
  - When to use: Deciding whether to invest in a single product or build a multi-product ecosystem
  - How: Ask whether the value depends on inter-user relationships or on the quality of a single deliverable

- **Multi-Layer Evaluation** (from the social-trading example): Don't evaluate signals at face value. Build layers—first rank performers by past performance, then let users allocate capital, then create incentives for transparency.
  - When to use: Designing markets where quality is hard to observe upfront
  - How: Stack observability mechanisms until good actors are distinguishable from bad ones

## Key Concepts
- **Search/Discovery Dilution**: When catalog grows faster than search relevance, discovery value collapses
- **Copy Trading Blind Spot**: Platforms claiming to show performance but requiring users to "open hands" lack genuine data network effects
- **Relationship-Based Networks**: Network effects only exist when the value comes from inter-human connections, not just aggregate usage
- **Project Economy**: Industries (film, many games) where talent assembles temporarily around a deliverable and disperses afterward
- **Iterative Validation Cycles**: The pattern of testing ideas rapidly (three game prototypes in this case) to find market fit before committing resources
- **Primary Market Capital Formation**: Channeling idle cash into productive business investment rather than consumption-driven price inflation
- **Observability Gap**: The problem where you can't tell whether a signal-provider is skilled or lucky without historical, transparent data

## Mental Models
- More products ≠ better experience; **better discovery** = better experience. The Basalam case shows that when every search returns hundreds of indistinguishable carrots, the entire value proposition evaporates.
- A platform is not a collection of products—it's a system where user-to-user relationships create compounding value. Without those relationships, you have a project.
- Data network effects are real and powerful, but they require transparency. If users hide their trades, you can't learn who's skilled.

## Anti-patterns
- **Feature-led expansion**: Adding products, channels, or services hoping growth will happen automatically. The result is always the same—discovery degrades, churn increases.
- **Confusing scale with network effects**: Having thousands of users does not equal network effects. If users don't interact with each other in meaningful ways, you have distribution, not a network.
- **Ranking without observability**: Publishing leaderboards or rankings when users haven't committed to revealing their strategies. Any ranking based on self-reported or hidden performance is meaningless.
- **Project-company thinking for platform ambitions**: Trying to build a platform (multi-developer, multi-game, publisher infrastructure) while treating it like a single-product studio. The economics don't match.

## Key Takeaways
1. Product growth must be paired with discovery growth—or you're actively destroying value.
2. The Basalam lesson: more inventory without better search is worse UX, not neutral.
3. Data network effects are the hardest moat to build and the strongest when you have them—but they require transparency.
4. Platform vs project is not a branding choice; it's a fundamental architecture decision that determines hiring, fundraising, and timeline.
5. Multi-layer evaluation beats single-metric evaluation. Rank, observe, then allocate.
6. Copy trading fails when it relies on reputation rather than verified, transparent track records.
7. Iterate fast, kill early, and commit only after you've found the one thing that works.
