# Patterns

## Principle-First Thinking
**When** — Entering new markets, tackling cross-industry challenges, building from scratch, or facing existential crises where standard playbooks fail.
**How** — Strip away inherited assumptions until you reach undisputed facts; rebuild from there. Ask "what must be true?" before asking "what do the numbers say?" Listen to experts, but act based on your fundamental belief.
**Trade-offs** — Requires intellectual independence; risks ignoring domain expertise entirely. Best paired with Design Thinking to avoid blind spots.

## Design Thinking
**When** — When you suspect the problem statement itself is flawed or inherited; when teams are optimizing around the wrong problem.
**How** — Ask "who decided this is the right problem?" Push past symptoms by drilling into root causes. Frame the problem, not the solution.
**Trade-offs** — Slow initial progress; can feel like "just talking" without immediate output.

## Status Quo Challenge
**When** — When incremental improvements aren't producing meaningful impact; evaluating legacy systems, processes, or business models.
**How** — Question every "this is how it's always been done." Ask who benefits from the current arrangement and whether that benefit still justifies it.
**Trade-offs** — Risks alienating stakeholders invested in the status quo; requires courage to challenge consensus.

## Zero Problem
**When** — Diagnosing why a system won't launch or scale.
**How** — Strip away symptoms until you find the single constraint preventing movement (liquidity for marketplaces, trust for platforms).
**Trade-offs** — Hard to identify correctly; requires deep systems understanding.

## Danger-Sajo / Danger-Sayo
**When** — Recognizing systemic failure as a founding moment.
**How** — Reframe breakdowns as market signals rather than obstacles.
**Trade-offs** — Requires reframing capability; can feel counterintuitive.

## Problem Framing
**When** — Before proposing solutions; when stuck on incremental improvements.
**How** — Ask "what is the real problem?" before "what is the solution?" Reframe over solve.
**Trade-offs** — Takes longer upfront; requires willingness to abandon assumed problem definitions.

## Second-Order Thinking
**When** — Evaluating proposed solutions; avoiding unintended consequences.
**How** — Ask "what happens after that?" to uncover hidden consequences and unintended side effects.
**Trade-offs** — Can lead to analysis paralysis; balance with decisive action.

## Reframing Over Solving
**When** — When the problem statement feels wrong or inherited.
**How** — Change the problem definition before proposing fixes.
**Trade-offs** — Risks scope creep; requires clarity on what constitutes the real problem.

## Geographic Constraint Reframing
**When** — Facing local limitations perceived as disadvantages.
**How** — Treat local limitations as competitive advantages rather than excuses.
**Trade-offs** — Works best when paired with real differentiation.

## Decision-Making Muscle
**When** — Building organizational capacity for better choices.
**How** — Quality of decisions scales with volume of decisions practiced—not just frequency, but deliberate practice.
**Trade-offs** — Takes time to build; requires psychological safety for trial/error.

## Principle-First Evaluation
**When** — Comparing competitors, evaluating investment opportunities, diagnosing why a company is winning or losing.
**How** — Map a company's operational choices → financial outcomes → strategic positioning. Test each principle against real metrics (gross margin, cross margin, P/S ratio, gross unit economics).
**Trade-offs** — Requires access to detailed financial data; can miss qualitative factors.

## Value Proposition Shift
**When** — Designing platform strategies, avoiding feature bloat.
**How** — Start with one core value. Let network effects change what customers value. Evolve gradually.
**Trade-offs** — Risk of abandoning original positioning; requires patience.

## Bundling
**When** — Platform strategy, ecosystem building.
**How** — Identify which values naturally compound. Bundle them rather than optimizing them separately.
**Trade-offs** — Risk of feature bloat; harder to manage complexity.

## Flywheel vs Friction
**When** — Deciding where to focus resources.
**How** — Keep every initiative aligned with the same rotational direction. Don't launch new features that slow the core loop.
**Trade-offs** — Hard to identify the true flywheel; requires discipline to say no to interesting-but-unaligned initiatives.

## Data as Moat
**When** — Deciding what to build first, what to prioritize.
**How** — Every feature should either generate more data or leverage existing data to create better user experiences.
**Trade-offs** — Privacy concerns; requires infrastructure investment.

## Tit-for-Tat
**When** — Repeated interactions with known opponents.
**How** — Cooperate first; mirror opponent's previous move. Nice, direct, no grudges.
**Trade-offs** — Vulnerable to noise/misunderstanding; pair with Generous TFT in noisy environments.

## Generous Tit-for-Tat
**When** — Environments with noise/false signals.
**How** — After opponent defects, sometimes still cooperate to avoid lock-in.
**Trade-offs** — Risk of exploitation; requires calibration of forgiveness frequency.

## Test-and-Defect
**When** — Early-stage market entry.
**How** — Test opponent's type, then follow their pattern.
**Trade-offs** — Risk of signaling weakness during test phase.

## Platform Thinking
**When** — Scaling beyond resource constraints; building network effects.
**How** — Empower and inspire others to deliver value rather than delivering it yourself. Build connections rather than vertical control.
**Trade-offs** — Harder to control quality; requires trust in decentralized systems.

## Infinite Game
**When** — Playing repeated interactions; building long-term value.
**How** — Cooperate first; mirror opponent's previous move.
**Trade-offs** — Requires patience; conflicts with short-term pressure.

## Finite Game
**When** — One-shot or terminal interactions.
**How** — Recognize when cooperation is impossible; compete directly.
**Trade-offs** — Burns bridges; limits future options.

## Value Chain Upward Trace
**When** — Analyzing market positioning, avoiding conflating internal efficiency with industry-wide structural advantages.
**How** — Map the industry chain to identify complementors; map the corporate chain to optimize core vs. support functions.
**Trade-offs** — Requires deep industry knowledge; risk of oversimplification.

## Network-First Growth
**When** — Entering marketplace or platform businesses where liquidity drives value.
**How** — Secure end-users and channels first, then build the product, and finally scale via M&A or innovation platforms.
**Trade-offs** — Capital intensive upfront; risk of building product no one wants.

## Complementor Reframing
**When** — Stuck in zero-sum competition to unlock collaborative moats.
**How** — Identify overlapping but non-competing value streams; shift from competition to cooperation.
**Trade-offs** — Risk of enabling competitors; requires careful boundary-setting.

## Big Picture Thinking
**When** — Breaking out of the specialist trap common in engineering backgrounds.
**How** — See beyond micro-details to understand system-wide dynamics.
**Trade-offs** — Risk of missing important details; balance with specialist input.

## Core vs Support Functions
**When** — Determining what to keep in-house vs. outsource.
**How** — Core functions deliver primary customer value; support functions enable it.
**Trade-offs** — Blurred boundaries; requires ongoing reassessment.

## Bargaining Power Shift
**When** — Flipping power dynamics in negotiations.
**How** — Identify proximity to end-user as leverage point.
**Trade-offs** — May require sacrificing short-term revenue for long-term positioning.

## Principle-First (Supplier Empowerment)
**When** — Designing platform architecture or evaluating strategic investments.
**How** — Test every design decision against "Does this help suppliers win?"
**Trade-offs** — May reduce short-term take rate; requires long-term view.

## Platform vs Aggregator
**When** — Classifying competitors, positioning your own offering.
**How** — Ask whether the entity makes others more valuable or just redistributes existing value.
**Trade-offs** — Hard to distinguish in practice; requires nuanced judgment.

## Supply-Side Differentiation
**When** — Marketplaces, multi-sided platforms, e-commerce ecosystems.
**How** — Custom shop pages, branded experiences, supplier storytelling, unique packaging/branding rights.
**Trade-offs** — Higher complexity; requires ongoing supplier support.

## Habit Formation
**When** — Product roadmap decisions, feature prioritization, competitive positioning.
**How** — Identify the habit loop, design for compounding engagement, resist feature fragmentation.
**Trade-offs** — Risk of addiction-design; ethical considerations.

## Authentic Purpose
**When** — Founding a company, facing the cold start problem, or navigating a crisis where motivation is low.
**How** — Reflect on what genuinely wakes you up; identify core values; ensure the purpose is spiritual/meaningful, not purely material. Validate using the T-shirt Test.
**Trade-offs** — Hard to articulate clearly; can feel vague or abstract.

## Cold Start Problem
**When** — Launching a new marketplace or platform.
**How** — Solve with authentic purpose, targeted seeding, and community-building flywheel.
**Trade-offs** — Requires patience; may need creative subsidies.

## Community-Building Flywheel
**When** — Building supply-side density.
**How** — Start with a problem → gather people → activate them → convert them into partners/sellers. Leverage existing networks rather than cold acquisition.
**Trade-offs** — Slow initial growth; hard to scale community organically.

## Empowerment vs Control
**When** — Designing platform governance rules.
**How** — Empower users to decide yields better outcomes than deciding for them.
**Trade-offs** — Risk of inconsistent quality; requires reputation systems.

## Decentralization Paradox
**When** — Balancing quality with flexibility.
**How** — Intuitively, centralized control seems superior for quality—but in practice, decentralized systems often deliver better results because they harness distributed intelligence.
**Trade-offs** — Harder to maintain standards; requires strong community norms.

## Centralized ↔ Decentralized Spectrum
**When** — Designing platform governance rules.
**How** — Map each rule onto the spectrum and test what happens as you move.
**Trade-offs** — No perfect point; requires ongoing tuning.

## Principle-First (Crisis)
**When** — Facing unprecedented crises where standard industry playbooks fail.
**How** — Identify the core problem, listen to experts, but act based on your fundamental belief of what must happen to survive.
**Trade-offs** — High stakes; risk of acting on wrong principles.

## Clean Lens (Beginner's Mind)
**When** — Solving complex, systemic issues where traditional methods have failed.
**How** — Rely on first principles and interdisciplinary insights rather than pure domain expertise to bypass status quo defense mechanisms.
**Trade-offs** — Can miss important domain-specific constraints.

## Silicon Valley Flywheel
**When** — Explaining why ecosystems cluster; motivating capital recycling
**How** — Map the loop: talent → startups → exits → angels → talent again. Each successful exit feeds the next generation.
**Trade-offs** — Works in high-density clusters; fails in thin markets without critical mass.

## Concrete-to-Abstract Communication
**When** — Pitching mixed audiences; teaching complex concepts; writing for broad readership
**How** — Anchor with real cases/membership, then elevate to principles. Reverse for expert audiences.
**Trade-offs** — Too much abstraction loses novices; too much concreteness bores experts.

## Principle-First Decision Making
**When** — Uncertainty-heavy decisions; repeated choices with shared context; cross-functional alignment
**How** — Explicitly define operating principles upfront. Use them as axioms to interpret data, not replace data.
**Trade-offs** — Principles must be tested and revisable. Rigid adherence becomes dogma.

## Value-First Monetization (Gates Line)
**When** — Early-stage products; platform/ecosystem plays; marketplaces
**How** — Ship value → observe usage → identify extractable assets → layer monetization. Ensure users earn more from platform than platform earns from them.
**Trade-offs** — Delayed revenue; requires patience. Monetization must emerge organically or it feels extractive.

## Functionality-First Innovation
**When** — Evaluating new tech (LLMs, blockchain) for product use; avoiding tech-lock-in
**How** — Ask "What problem does it solve?" not "What is it?" Compose with existing tools.
**Trade-offs** — Risks missing genuinely novel capabilities; some breakthroughs don't compose cleanly.

## Platform vs. Project Mentality
**When** — Choosing between custom feature builds and platform infrastructure
**How** — Ask: "Can I build a system others build on, or am I delivering a one-off?" Apply Core vs. Long-Tail Rule (80/20).
**Trade-offs** — Platforms take longer to show value; projects deliver quick wins but lock you into feature creep.

## Status Quo Challenge (Independent Thinking)
**When** — Facing dissent on product direction; launching radical innovations; pushing against incumbents
**How** — Listen to all inputs, synthesize rationally, decide independently. Stand firm against backlash.
**Trade-offs** — Can look like stubbornness. Must distinguish from dictatorship of opinion.

## Design Thinking Responsibility
**When** — Users reject a product; communities don't participate; adoption stalls
**How** — Own the failure. If users don't get it, the design is broken. Dig into needs, biases, motivations—not stated desires.
**Trade-offs** — Requires humility; can conflict with conviction-driven leadership.

## Growth Mindset Team Development
**When** — Hiring; performance reviews; building team capabilities; resolving conflicts
**How** — Judge products, not people. Create IDPs addressing specific gaps (abstraction, communication, logic). Give time to digest new mental models.
**Trade-offs** — Takes more time upfront. Some people truly can't grow into certain roles.

## Belief-Driven Strategy
**When** — Competing against incumbents; identifying asymmetric opportunities
**How** — Find competencies incumbents dismiss ("reverse belief"). Build around what they won't pursue.
**Trade-offs** — Requires conviction that may look irrational. High risk if belief is wrong.

## Value-Add Interaction Design
**When** — Every stakeholder touchpoint: VC meetings, hiring, customer support, partnerships
**How** — Ensure every interaction leaves the other person better than they arrived. Even failed transactions become relationships.
**Trade-offs** — Hard to measure ROI. Requires discipline when incentives push toward extraction.

## Divergent Marketing
**When** — Launching new products; building brand; community engagement
**How** — Find real value → build it → develop it → *then* market. Don't slap marketing on empty.
**Trade-offs** — Slower go-to-market. Can't rely on clever messaging to mask weak product.

## Narrative-Driven Purpose
**When** — Company culture; fundraising; recruiting; brand building
**How** — Tell origin stories that create emotional connection (e.g., Shopify's Pacific island story). Stories sell purpose; logic sells features.
**Trade-offs** — Authenticity matters; fabricated stories backfire. Hard to scale emotionally.
